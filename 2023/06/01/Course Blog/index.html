AI and Alzheimer's: Diagnosis and Ethics

SN:21110089
Course Blog Course Blog

Posted on:Jun 1, 2023

Introduction

============

Background of Alzheimer’s Disease

With the relentless advance of medical technology and the concurrent decline in birth rates, the demographic profile in the United Kingdom is undergoing a noteworthy transformation towards an aged society[1]. Projections indicate that by the year 2050, a full one-fourth of the UK population will be aged 65 or above[2], underscoring the necessity for the healthcare sector to evolve in accordance with this shift to safeguard the nation’s future prosperity. The aging of the population brings with it a set of unique healthcare challenges[3], necessitating a paradigm shift towards more targeted and effective disease prevention strategies and treatment approaches.This evolution is not confined to the UK alone; globally, societies are witnessing an unprecedented increase in the aging population[4], which is reshaping the public health landscape. It’s predicted that by 2050, the number of individuals aged 60 and above will surpass two billion worldwide, accounting for over 20% of the global population[5]. This demographic transition necessitates a rethinking of healthcare strategies on a global scale, which must now cater to an increasingly older population with specific and complex health needs.

In the current healthcare landscape, Alzheimer’s Disease (AD), an irreversible neurodegenerative condition, poses an increasingly significant health challenge. This ailment, characterized by a progressive decline in cognitive abilities, impairment in day-to-day activities, and the manifestation of various neuropsychiatric symptoms or behavior changes, casts a long shadow on the quality of life of older adults[6]. Importantly, AD has earned the notorious distinction of being the leading cause of dementia among the elderly population.

The World Health Organization’s data illustrates the daunting scale of this global health crisis. At present, it is estimated that around 50 million individuals worldwide are grappling with dementia, with AD constituting approximately 60-70% of these cases[7]. However, the AD epidemic isn’t merely a health issue; it also has significant economic ramifications. The current annual cost burden of dementia on the UK economy stands at a staggering £26 billion[8], a figure that poses serious concerns for the nation’s economic health.

These economic implications become even more severe when considering the predicted increase in dementia prevalence in line with demographic shifts. The number of individuals afflicted by dementia in the UK is forecasted to surge to 1.5 million by the year 2040[9], suggesting that without effective interventions, the economic burden is set to rise exponentially. Moreover, the human cost of AD is incalculable. Beyond the cognitive decline, AD patients often suffer from emotional distress and psychological discomfort[10], largely due to the loss of independence and the frustration associated with declining abilities. It also poses enormous challenges to caregivers, often leading to caregiver burnout due to the physical, emotional, and financial strains of providing long-term care[11].

The Challenge of Alzheimer’s Disease: Early Detection

Despite the significant impact of Alzheimer’s disease on individuals and society, methods to halt its progression are currently non-existent[12]. This is primarily due to the obscure nature of the underlying disease mechanisms that still puzzle the scientific community[13]. Over the past few decades, the amyloid hypothesis, proposing the accumulation of beta-amyloid plaques as the primary culprit in Alzheimer’s disease, has been a cornerstone of research, with numerous clinical trials geared towards reducing the amyloid burden in the brain. Yet, the results have consistently fallen short[14], casting a shadow of doubt over this hypothesis and illustrating the stubborn complexity of this neurodegenerative condition.

On one hand, clinical trials for the treatment of Alzheimer’s disease face significant obstacles, including staggering financial costs and alarmingly high failure rates. On the other hand, only two types of drugs - cholinesterase inhibitors and N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor antagonists - have been approved. However, these treatments can only alleviate symptoms and cannot halt the progression of the disease[13]. In addition to this, the onset of Alzheimer’s disease is often hidden, and the boundary between normal aging symptoms and early signs of Alzheimer’s often becomes blurred[15]. Symptoms associated with the aging process, such as forgetfulness and a decline in mental acuity, may be early indicators of this neurodegenerative change. Moreover, the clinical diagnosis of Alzheimer’s disease typically involves a time-consuming and expensive process[16], adding additional financial and emotional stress to patients and their families. At this stage, irreversible neuronal damage has usually begun, and the individual has already been caught in an irreversible disease process. This reality underscores a key challenge in the management of Alzheimer’s disease: early diagnosis. Early and accurate diagnosis not only helps manage patients better, but also opens up the possibility of intervening at the stage where potential treatments are most effective. This challenge emphasizes the need for better diagnostic tools and protocols for early detection, necessitating innovation and research in this direction.

The progression of Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is typically characterized by a shift across three clinical stages[17], each with distinct symptoms and prognoses that reflect the disease’s unyielding advance through the brain. It is worth understanding these stages in depth, as they present unique challenges and opportunities in the context of early diagnosis and intervention. The first stage, known as the presymptomatic stage, is arguably the most elusive. Despite the absence of overt cognitive symptoms, this stage sees the first whisperings of AD’s pathological changes in the brain. Insidious behavioral and emotional changes can surface, alongside subtle impairments in memory function. Yet, individuals during this stage remain ostensibly cognitively normal, their AD pathology hidden behind a facade of normality. It is at this stage that the disease’s covert nature underscores the crucial need for early detection tools that can identify risk markers for AD even before cognitive symptoms begin to emerge.Following the presymptomatic stage is Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI), the second stage. The transition to this stage sees the emergence of more noticeable cognitive problems, particularly concerning situational cognition and memory. Though these individuals do not meet the criteria for dementia, their daily life begins to be impacted by these cognitive deficits. Over time, these deficits become more profound, with memory, reasoning, and language skills gradually deteriorating. Concurrently, personality changes and decline in functional abilities are observed, slowly marching towards dementia. Statistically, around 15% of patients at this stage progress to the next stage. However, the specific triggers and mechanisms of this progression are still unclear, underlining the need for ongoing research. The third stage, dementia, signifies the full manifestation of Alzheimer’s disease, characterized by severe cognitive impairment rendering the individual incapable of independent living. Assistance is often required for daily activities, further straining emotional and financial resources. At this stage, the individual’s personality and cognitive abilities are profoundly affected, making interactions and care increasingly challenging. Current medical treatments, at their best, provide only palliative relief at this stage, underscoring the importance of early diagnosis and intervention.Given this understanding of AD progression, it becomes abundantly clear that diagnosing and intervening before the onset of full-blown Alzheimer’s disease is a mounting challenge.

AI and ML in Early Alzheimer’s Detection

Machine Learning (ML) and Artificial Intelligence (AI) technologies hold tremendous promise in meeting the early detection challenge associated with Alzheimer’s Disease (AD). This derives from the inherent capabilities of these computational tools in handling high-dimensional, heterogeneous, and complex health data, enabling them to uncover patterns and make predictions that might elude traditional analytical approaches.

Firstly, ML and AI excel in handling the high-dimensional and complex nature of data associated with AD. The disease’s onset and progression involve multifaceted and interconnected factors[18], including genetic variables, biomarkers, cognitive test scores, neuroimaging data, and lifestyle factors. The interactions among these elements are nonlinear and complex, thus posing significant challenges for traditional statistical methods. On the other hand, ML and AI algorithms are designed to thrive in such high-dimensional and complex environments, effectively capturing intricate patterns and associations in the data.

Moreover, AI and ML technologies are exceptional in their ability to handle heterogeneity[19]. Alzheimer’s is an inherently heterogeneous disease, manifesting differently among individuals in terms of symptoms, progression rate, and response to treatment. This heterogeneity is reflective of the diverse and variable data that patients generate across multiple modalities (e.g., genomic, proteomic, neuroimaging). AI and ML are capable of integrating and analyzing this multifaceted data on a scale that human cognition cannot match. They can unearth hidden patterns and relationships within the data, allowing for a more nuanced understanding of the disease and its progression in different individuals. By capturing the heterogeneity of AD, AI and ML models can lead to more accurate diagnosis, tailored treatment plans, and improved patient outcomes. Thus, they hold considerable promise in addressing the challenge of early detection and treatment customization in Alzheimer’s disease.

Another crucial advantage of ML and AI technologies is their potential for scalability and cost-effectiveness. Traditional diagnostic tools for AD, such as lumbar punctures, are often expensive, invasive, and cannot be feasibly scaled to screen large populations[20]. In contrast, ML and AI models can be trained on large datasets and then deployed to make predictions on new data at a relatively low cost. This scalability can be instrumental in enabling population-wide screening for AD, thus catching more cases at the early stages when intervention is most likely to be successful.

Additionally, AI and ML models are capable of continuous learning. As more data becomes available, these models can be retrained or fine-tuned, thereby continually improving their performance. This capacity for ongoing learning is particularly relevant in the context of a disease like Alzheimer’s, where our understanding is continually evolving, and new biomarkers and risk factors are constantly being identified.

In summary, the complex, high-dimensional, and heterogeneous nature of Alzheimer’s disease, combined with the need for cost-effective and scalable healthcare solutions, demands innovative approaches. Machine Learning and Artificial Intelligence technologies provide an unparalleled opportunity to meet these challenges head-on. These powerful tools can process and understand vast and diverse data sets, facilitating early diagnosis and personalized treatment strategies.

Implementing AI and ML for Early Alzheimer’s Diagnosis

======================================================

Modeling Early Diagnosis

The protracted development of Alzheimer’s disease, often spanning over a decade[21], generates temporal data that fluctuates and evolves over time. In the quest for early diagnosis, the aim is to utilize this data to predict potential AD development in patients. Accordingly, the early diagnosis task naturally maps onto a classification problem within the machine learning discipline, where different stages of cognitive states serve as distinct classes. However, the granularity of these classifications can vary based on specific diagnostic objectives.

At a high level, a binary classification can be used to distinguish between cognitively normal (CN) individuals and those diagnosed with Alzheimer’s disease (AD). This level of differentiation often yields higher accuracy due to more pronounced differences between the classes. However, as we move towards the early stages of the disease, the classification task becomes increasingly intricate. The differentiation between CN individuals and those with mild cognitive impairment (MCI) presents a considerable challenge due to subtler differences in symptoms and cognitive function.

Moreover, the challenge escalates when distinguishing between MCI and AD, and even more so when classifying between progressive MCI (pMCI) and stable MCI (sMCI), or between amnestic MCI (aMCI) and non-amnestic MCI (naMCI)[15]. Each of these classification tasks reflects the nuanced progression and variants of the disease, adding to their complexity (Rémi Cuingnet et al., 2011). For an even more comprehensive perspective, classification problems can extend to three-class tasks (e.g., CN vs. MCI vs. AD) or even four-class tasks (e.g., CN vs. sMCI vs. pMCI vs. AD).

These classification challenges underscore the intricate complexity and heterogeneity of Alzheimer’s disease progression. Given the subtle and slow emergence of early symptoms, accurately identifying patient classes becomes paramount for enabling early intervention and personalized treatment strategies. To address these challenges, the development and refinement of robust, generalizable classification models capable of effectively distinguishing between varying stages of disease progression are needed.

Data Sources in Alzheimer’s Early Detection

Addressing the early detection challenge of Alzheimer’s disease necessitates the leveraging of diverse data sources for model training. Such an approach facilitates a comprehensive understanding of disease progression, thereby aiding in the accurate diagnosis of the condition.

At the forefront of these data sources is neuroimaging, with magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) being a principal player. MRI provides detailed structural and functional insights into the brain, capturing an array of information including brain morphology, volume, and connectivity patterns[22]. This level of detail facilitates the identification of subtle brain changes associated with Alzheimer’s disease progression. In particular, the examination of regions such as the hippocampus and entorhinal cortex, known to be affected in the early stages of Alzheimer’s, enables the identification of neuroanatomical changes indicative of disease progression. Despite the richness of the insights MRI provides[23], its high cost limits the volume of available data. Rarely do datasets include more than a thousand subjects, making data collection a significant challenge.

Complementing neuroimaging data, medical records offer a treasure trove of historical information integral to the early detection process. These records encompass diverse data types such as cognitive assessments, medical diagnoses, medication history, and lifestyle factors[24]. Longitudinal medical records allow the tracking of cognitive decline over time, thus enabling the identification of disease patterns and trends indicative of Alzheimer’s. The wealth of insights obtained from these records enables a more accurate assessment of an individual’s disease risk and progression rate.

Exploring alternative avenues, speech and text analysis has emerged as a promising approach for early detection. Alzheimer’s disease progression precipitates changes in language and communication abilities, making speech patterns and written text viable markers for cognitive decline[25]. By applying natural language processing techniques to analyze linguistic and semantic features in speech and written text samples, researchers can identify subtle cognitive impairments and distinguish individuals with Alzheimer’s disease from healthy individuals or those with other dementia forms. Factors like increased pauses, word-finding difficulties, and syntactic errors serve as linguistic markers for the disease. Compared to MRI, acquiring speech and text datasets is considerably more accessible and cost-effective.

In recent times, the analysis of genomic data has gained traction as a promising approach for early detection. Some genetic variations have been linked with an increased risk of developing Alzheimer’s disease. Genomic studies aim to identify these specific genetic markers or risk factors, thereby enhancing our understanding of the disease’s underlying biological mechanisms[26]. By analyzing an individual’s genomic profile, researchers can identify genetic variants related to Alzheimer’s disease susceptibility, thereby facilitating more accurate risk assessment and potentially paving the way for personalized interventions and treatments.

Techniques for Mitigating Data Scarcity

Facing the task of early diagnosis of Alzheimer’s disease, the scarcity of data becomes a major challenge. The collection of data related to Alzheimer’s disease is both expensive and time-consuming, thus making the application of deep learning to limited data a remarkably challenging task. Within the realm of machine learning and artificial intelligence, several techniques can help mitigate this issue, typically falling within the purview of data augmentation and transfer learning.

Data augmentation is one of the most common approaches to overcome the scarcity of data. This technique involves generating new data samples from existing ones by applying a variety of transformations that preserve the underlying information. For instance, in the context of MRI data for Alzheimer’s disease, augmentation could involve introducing small rotations, shifts, or flips to the images, creating new data samples that retain the same class labels. This process effectively increases the volume of training data, enhances the robustness of the model, and helps alleviate overfitting. Another example of data augmentation in the context of text data is the use of synonyms to replace words in sentences, thereby preserving the original meaning while creating new data instances.

Transfer learning is another powerful technique to mitigate the impact of limited data. In transfer learning, models pre-trained on large datasets are fine-tuned for the task of interest. These models typically learn generalizable features from the large source dataset and then apply them to a smaller target dataset. This approach is particularly effective when the source task and the target task are similar. For example, a model pre-trained on brain MRI scans could be fine-tuned for early detection of Alzheimer’s disease. Similarly, natural language processing models pre-trained on large text corpora could be fine-tuned to analyze speech patterns indicative of cognitive decline.

Furthermore, the exploration of synthetic data generation can be pursued. Techniques such as Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [27]can generate synthetic data that reflects the distribution of real data, potentially expanding the training dataset. For instance, GANs can be used to generate synthetic MRI images or speech samples exhibiting features of Alzheimer’s disease, supplementing the limited available data.Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) are an innovative deep learning approach designed for generating synthetic data. They are composed of two parts: a generator network and a discriminator network.

The Generator network is responsible for creating new synthetic data. It takes a random noise vector as input and processes it through a series of layers, often convolutional layers, to output synthetic data with the same structure as the real data. Initially, the generator produces random outputs, but it gradually improves its ability to create more realistic synthetic data over time during the training process.

The Discriminator network, on the other hand, takes both real data and synthetic data (produced by the generator) as input. Its job is to classify if the input data is real (from the original dataset) or fake (generated by the generator). It is usually a binary classifier that outputs a value between 0 and 1, representing the probability of the input being real data.

During the training process, the generator and the discriminator play a two-player minimax game, where the generator tries to produce data that the discriminator cannot distinguish from real data, while the discriminator tries to get better at distinguishing real data from fake data. In other words, the generator aims to maximize the probability that the discriminator makes a mistake, and the discriminator aims to minimize this probability. This dynamic leads the generator to produce increasingly realistic data as training progresses.

Despite its elegance, training GANs can be tricky as it’s challenging to maintain a balance between the generator and the discriminator. If either network becomes too powerful relative to the other, the training process may fail, leading to poor results. With careful design and parameter tuning, however, GANs have demonstrated their ability to produce strikingly realistic synthetic data in various domains.

In summary, while data scarcity poses significant challenges to the application of AI and ML technologies in Alzheimer’s disease, several approaches including data augmentation, transfer learning, and synthetic data generation can help address this issue, thereby facilitating the development of effective models for early detection and diagnosis.

Image-based Models for Early Diagnosis

Given that the current data for Alzheimer’s disease primarily encompasses images and text, computer vision models adept at processing image data and natural language processing models skilled in text handling can both be deployed for Alzheimer’s diagnosis. For image data, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Transformer-based models have emerged as mainstream frameworks for image classification tasks.

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) excel in extracting valuable information from neuroimaging data, such as MRI scans. Two prominent exemplars within this category include the Residual Networks (ResNet)[28] and EfficientNet[29]. ResNet has made profound impacts on deep learning through the introduction of residual and “skip” connections that enable the training of models with greater depth without succumbing to the challenges of vanishing or exploding gradients. Such a network is aptly equipped to discern subtle neurodegenerative alterations, such as atrophy in the hippocampus and frontal cortex, which are vital for the early detection of Alzheimer’s. On the other hand, EfficientNet enhances learning by concurrently scaling the network’s depth, width, and resolution, effectively extracting multiscale features within neuroimaging data. Despite its superior performance and efficiency, it demands higher computational resources and carefully tuned data preprocessing.

Transformer-based models, leveraging self-attention mechanisms, capture long-range dependencies in input data, proving invaluable for detecting early Alzheimer’s through variations in patients’ language and cognitive abilities. Vision Transformer (ViT)[30] applies Transformer models to visual tasks, offering a competitive edge over CNNs in handling intricate and nuanced patterns within neuroimaging data. However, ViTs require extensive training datasets and bear high computational costs during training and inference stages. The Swin Transformer[31], a Transformer model purpose-built for visual tasks, captures more complex features in neuroimaging data, reducing computational complexity. Nevertheless, akin to ViTs, Swin Transformers also require plentiful training data and carry high computational overhead.

The choice between CNNs and Transformer-based models hinges on the specific task, the availability of data, and the computational resources. Practical implementations often necessitate trade-offs and selections based on specific needs and constraints, aiming to strike a balance between model complexity, interpretability, and performance.

Text-based Models for Early Diagnosis

Given the critical role of language and cognitive abilities in the diagnosis of Alzheimer’s disease, speech and semantic analysis also serve as viable methods for early detection of Alzheimer’s. In the realm of Natural Language Processing (NLP), Transformer-based models, specifically BERT[32] and DeBERTa[33], have demonstrated excellent performance across various tasks, thereby marking them as highly suitable for Alzheimer’s detection.

BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) has revolutionized the field of NLP by introducing an attention mechanism that encapsulates context bidirectionally, enhancing the model’s comprehension of semantic relationships within text data. Specifically, when applied to the early detection of Alzheimer’s disease, BERT can be trained to discern subtle changes in language use, sentence structure, and other linguistic indicators that might suggest cognitive decline. However, BERT’s main limitation lies in its inability to manage long-term dependencies due to its fixed-length context window, potentially causing it to miss broader contextual information.

DeBERTa (Decoding-enhanced BERT with disentangled attention), an enhancement over the BERT model, disentangles the intermingled semantic meanings present in BERT’s attention mechanism. This model introduces a decoding-enhanced representation that amplifies the dot-product attention into the Euclidean space, effectively balancing the global and fine-grained information. As a result, DeBERTa could potentially be more efficient in analyzing speech or written text data from Alzheimer’s patients, extracting complex patterns and identifying indicators of cognitive decline. However, similar to BERT, DeBERTa also requires substantial computational resources for training and may struggle with managing long-range dependencies.

Ethics

Based on the Belmont Report, there are five principles that must be adhered to in the medical field: respect, beneficence, justice, non-maleficence, and autonomy[34]. Therefore, when attempting to apply artificial intelligence technology in the medical field, careful consideration should be given as to whether these principles might be violated. In addition to this, ethical issues arising from the limitations of this technology in clinical application should also be contemplated.

Data Collection and Protection

Collection and Protection of Data: In the application of Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) for the early detection of dementia, the collection and utilization of a large amount of personal data is indispensable. This may include personal health records, genetic information, lifestyle data, and more. Adhering to the principle of respect, the collection and use of this data must be highly transparent - patients and their families should clearly understand what their data is being used for and why it is needed. This transparency can be expressed through informed consent, but the complex use of data, such as secondary use or cross-linking of data, should also be considered. While the availability of anonymized datasets has always been a blessing of technological progress, there are still many challenges[35]. The principle of beneficence requires healthcare providers to “do no harm”; however, violating patient privacy can cause significant harm and potentially unexpected consequences[36]. Due to the vast demand for data in the field of artificial intelligence, and the scarcity of data available for early diagnosis of Alzheimer’s disease, it often requires integrating data from multiple institutions. Therefore, datasets must be shared outside the institutions that generated them. If improperly executed, such data sharing could increase the risk of data leakage. Everyone has the right to protect their personal information from abuse or misuse. Therefore, when collecting and processing sensitive medical data, the privacy rights of individuals must be respected and protected. For all collected and processed data, strict data security standards and protocols should be adhered to. This might include implementing mandatory security measures in terms of data storage and access to protect the data from unauthorized access or disclosure.

Bias

In the application of AI technology in the field of medical insurance, one crucial requirement is accuracy. However, AI technologies often bear certain biases, and the performance of models largely depends on the data used to train them. If the training data is imbalanced or biased, it might result in unfair predictions for certain groups, thus violating the principle of justice. Given the scarcity of data for Alzheimer’s disease, this issue is particularly severe. The bias in models primarily comes from four sources. Firstly, during the process of data annotation, personal preferences impact the fairness of the dataset. Secondly, the distribution of patient data acquired over different periods is not uniform. The timespan of various stages of Alzheimer’s disease is not identical, particularly the preclinical stage, which far exceeds the other two stages. Besides, some atypical minority symptoms may be unrecorded or only appear a few times in smaller datasets, leading to the model’s inability to identify such populations. Following this, there’s the inherent bias in the algorithms used in AI computations. Different models introduce different biases when training data. For instance, when an algorithm focuses on minimizing overall loss or the loss of the worst-performing category, the biases introduced are different. Lastly, there is bias introduced by the randomness in model training, mainly due to the stochasticity of initialization configurations when using AI technology and the uncertainty of training time. In other fields, such as recidivism and job applications, data-driven systems have shown signs of social bias and racial discrimination[37]. Therefore, it is of utmost importance to approach these biases with great care and deliberation. Rigorous testing, validation, and refinement of these models must be conducted to minimize such biases. Furthermore, there needs to be transparency about the limitations and potential biases of these models so that users and patients can make informed decisions. Developing methods for addressing bias and promoting fairness in AI models is an ongoing challenge and a critical area for future research in the field.

Impact of Automated Decision-making

Firstly, despite the tremendous potential of AI technology in early diagnosis of Alzheimer’s disease, interpretation and understanding of predictive outcomes are crucial for ethical conduct[38]. Due to the complexity of AI algorithms, they can generate highly accurate predictions, but their decision-making process often remains opaque. Hence, it is vital for patients and healthcare providers to comprehend the fundamental principles and rationale behind AI decisions. This transparency in explanation can help build trust in AI technology by patients and healthcare workers and ensure the decision-making process does not yield unfair or discriminatory outcomes.

Secondly, the interplay between AI decisions and human decisions also presents an ethical concern worth noting. Although AI technologies can provide efficient automated decision-making, the expertise and experience of healthcare professionals remain paramount. In the process of early diagnosis of Alzheimer’s, healthcare professionals should be able to understand the outputs of AI algorithms and use them as supporting tools for decision-making, not simply relying on the algorithm’s results. A harmonious collaboration between AI technology and human decision-making can enhance accuracy and efficiency and ensure patients receive optimal medical care.

Lastly, ensuring mechanisms for sufficient human review and correction of errors is a significant aspect of ethical safeguards. While AI algorithms can be trained and optimized on large datasets, they might still contain errors or biases. Therefore, healthcare institutions and researchers need to establish oversight and correction mechanisms to regularly review the accuracy and fairness of AI decisions and make timely amendments. This includes routine evaluation of algorithm performance and strategies for training and updating algorithms to ensure their robustness across diverse populations and settings.

Conclusion

==========

With societal aging, the number of people affected by Alzheimer’s disease is increasing, making the prevention, treatment, and alleviation of Alzheimer’s disease a pressing issue. Although the pathogenesis of Alzheimer’s disease is not yet clear and a cure remains a distant goal, early diagnosis holds great importance for Alzheimer’s disease. The objective of early diagnosis is to determine the stage of disease progression in patients, and hence it can be translated into a classification task in the field of machine learning, opening the way for the application of machine learning and AI technologies.

Due to the high cost of acquiring data such as neuroimaging, data scarcity is a common feature of datasets available for early diagnosis. In this context, overcoming the impact of limited data becomes crucial. This paper discusses how to break through the problem of data scarcity by using data augmentation, transfer learning, and generative adversarial networks, and explores how to apply computer vision models to process involved image data, such as MRI, and how to use natural language processing models to solve language recognition problems.

Unlike the field of deep learning, the healthcare industry should follow the principles of respect, beneficence, justice, non-maleficence, and autonomy. Collecting and sharing information against the patient’s wishes would violate the principles of respect, beneficence, and autonomy, while biases brought by AI technology might impact the principle of justice. Meanwhile, attention needs to be paid to the interpretability of the technology when integrating AI technology for automation in clinical settings. Although AI technology’s predictions are not entirely accurate, misdiagnosis of patients could have severe consequences and is intolerable. Hence, healthcare professionals need to understand the AI technology they are using to assist their diagnostic process. By following these principles and ensuring the interpretability of technology, sustainable development and application of AI technology in the field of early diagnosis of Alzheimer’s disease can be promoted.
